# Specialist Brief: Reframing Moves

## Research Scope

Techniques that change HOW you see the problem. Perspective shifts, constraint removal/addition, audience inversion, analogy bridging, "what if the opposite were true." Sources: de Bono (lateral thinking, provocation), design thinking (reframing, HMW questions), Eno (perspective-shifting cards), Munger (inversion).

## Key Findings

### 1. Provocation (de Bono's "Po")

De Bono's most powerful reframing tool. A provocation is a deliberately absurd, impossible, or counterfactual statement about the problem — not because it's true, but because it interrupts the established thinking pattern.

**Mechanism:** The brain follows self-organizing information patterns (like water flowing in channels). Provocation forces the brain out of its established channel. You don't evaluate the provocation — you use it as a stepping stone to reach ideas you couldn't have reached by logical progression.

**Canonical example:** Considering river pollution: "Po — the factory is downstream of itself." This absurd statement led to the insight that factories should be required to take their water intake from a point downstream of their own output. This actually became law in several countries.

**Software translation:** "Po — the user writes the documentation." Absurd on its face, but leads to: what if the product were self-documenting? What if user behavior *generated* the docs? What if error messages were so good they replaced documentation?

### 2. Frame-Storming (Design Thinking)

Instead of brainstorming solutions, brainstorm the *question itself*. The framing of a problem determines the solution space. A different question opens a different set of answers.

**Mechanism:** Most teams lock in their problem statement early and brainstorm within that frame. Frame-storming challenges the frame itself. Popularized through IDEO's design thinking methodology and the "How Might We" format.

**Canonical example:** "How should we plan a birthday party for Anna?" constrains to parties. Reframe to "How can we make Anna's day special?" — now the solution space includes non-party options.

**Software translation:** "How do we build a better search?" constrains to search UX. Reframe: "How might we help users find what they need without searching?" — now the solution space includes recommendations, contextual surfacing, progressive disclosure.

### 3. Inversion (Munger / Jacobi)

"Invert, always invert." Instead of asking how to succeed, ask what would guarantee failure — then avoid those things. Subtractive thinking surfaces risks and failure modes that additive thinking misses entirely.

**Mechanism:** The brain is better at recognizing danger than optimizing for success (evolutionary bias). Inversion leverages this by reframing "achieve X" as "avoid not-X." Charlie Munger attributes this to mathematician Carl Gustav Jacobi and calls it his most reliable thinking tool.

**Canonical example:** Instead of "how can I live a good life?" ask "what would guarantee a miserable life?" (Envy, resentment, unreliability, addiction.) Now avoid those. The path to a good life becomes clearer through subtraction.

**Software translation:** Instead of "how do we make onboarding great?" ask "what would make onboarding terrible?" (Require 15 fields before showing value. Hide the core feature behind three menus. Send a 10-step tutorial email. Force account creation before any interaction.) Now avoid each one systematically.

### 4. Audience Swap

Ask "what would [a specific, different person] do with this problem?" The other person can be real (a competitor, a user persona, a domain expert) or archetypal (a beginner, a child, someone hostile to the product).

**Mechanism:** Your own expertise creates blind spots — you can't see the problem fresh because you know too much about the constraints. Adopting another perspective bypasses your anchored assumptions. Eno's cards include variants: "What would your closest friend do?" and perspective-shifting provocations like "Look at the order in which you do things."

**Software translation:** "What would a 5-person startup build if they had this problem?" strips away enterprise complexity assumptions. "What would a user who hates our product say?" surfaces UX friction you've normalized. "What would someone from a completely different industry build?" breaks domain-specific pattern-matching.

### 5. Constraint Flip

Take the constraint you're working around and make it a feature. Or remove it entirely and see what changes.

**Mechanism:** Constraints shape the solution space invisibly. We optimize *within* constraints without questioning whether they should exist. Flipping a constraint (treating it as a requirement rather than a limitation, or removing it) reveals whether the constraint is load-bearing or vestigial.

**Software translation:** "We can't do real-time because of latency" — what if latency were a feature? (Batch processing surfaces patterns that real-time misses.) "We need backward compatibility" — what if we didn't? (What's the migration cost vs. the design debt cost?)

## Synthesis for Primer

The strongest reframing moves share a mechanism: they force the thinker to *exit the current frame* before generating solutions. The four approaches — provocation (absurd frame), frame-storming (question the question), inversion (reverse the goal), audience swap (borrow someone else's frame) — attack framing from different angles.

**Recommended moves for the primer:**
1. **Flip the Goal** (inversion) — most universally applicable, works in any context
2. **Question the Question** (frame-storming) — directly attacks the root of stuck-ness
3. **Borrow Eyes** (audience swap) — easiest to execute, lowest barrier
4. **State the Absurd** (provocation) — highest-ceiling technique, requires more practice

These four cover the reframing space without overlap. Each has a distinct trigger and a distinct mechanism.
